KILT is a protocol for self-sovereign data and interoperability built on top of the permissionless KILT blockchain. The core component of KILT is a digital identity protocol for 1. generating and managing decentralized identifiers (DIDs) 2. issuing and presenting digital verifiable credentials (VCs). In contrast to other centralized alternatives, KILT features self-sovereign data as well as revocable credentials anchored to the KILT blockchain. With KILT, anyone (or anything) can become an entity in the network by creating an identity or defining qualities or characteristics. The identity in KILT is created as a public-private key pair, which is then linked to a Decentralised Identifier (DID). In this case, the entity that created the DID is in full control.

KILT Decentralized Identifiers (DIDs)

Light DIDs: It is possible to generate and use KILT DIDs offline without any internet connection, which is called a light DID.

Full DIDs: In order to create a full DID, it is necessary to interact with the KILT blockchain. In order to create a full DID, it is necessary to interact with the KILT blockchain. It is therefore necessary to submit the DID creation process to a KILT address with enough funds to cover transaction fees and deposit requirements.

Claim Types (CTYPEs)

A claim's structure (i.e., its data model) is defined by CTypes, which are specific to KILT. Using CTypes, you can annotate and validate JSON documents using JSON Schema. Property types and types of properties are defined by the schema, e.g., string, number, object, etc.

CTYPEs can be created without permission. CTYPEs can be created by anyone and referenced on the KILT Blockchain.

CTypes can only be viewed in blockchain block histories, but their hashes are anchored to transaction histories.

Roles

Claimer(Holder)

Claims are generated by entities (individuals, organizations, objects) and stored by the client wallet software using a decentralized identifier. With KILT, the Claimer always signs the claim, proving that the claim was created by the same entity as the claim owner (i.e. the Claimer is always the origin of the claim). Each Claim type is defined by a Claim Type Definition (CTYPE), which is a well-defined structured data structure defining the properties and rules of a specified claim type. Claimer controls ● who to present the Credential to ● which Credential is presented and ● which parts of the Credential the Verifier can see. Attester (Issuer) In KILT Protocol, KILT tokens can be transferred to Attesters so that Claimers can pay for their work. During this process, the Attesters verify the validity of the Claimant's assertions. ****Attesters complete Credentials by cryptographically signing attestations and returning them to Claimers. A hash of credentials is then sent to the blockchain by Attesters. Attesters will only perform limited checks and confirm a limited range of properties. Attesters choose one or more Claim Types (CTYPEs) in line with these capabilities and offer to attest them on behalf of interested Claimers. There are various criteria that Claimers can use to choose an Attester (price, speed, credential types, number, and importance of Verifiers that trust their credentials). For Attesters, this creates new business opportunities. As another means of enhancing trust, attesters can cooperate with other attesters or delegate trust to other parties. Verifier

Credentials are created by the Verifier as he offers a service to the Claimers. In the absence of trust between Claimers and Verifiers, the Verifiers rely on the Attesters. Each Attester is chosen by the Verifier. Upon choosing Attesters, Claimers should ensure they are trusted by Verifiers and relevant to their use case. The Verifier can check if the same hash of the Credential is also present on the blockchain after receiving the Credential from the Claimer.

Verifiers identify Claimers through a Cryptographic Challenge. The KILT Blockchain uses Merkle Trees for the validation of Credentials with hashes retrieved from it, even if the Claimer reveals only parts of the Credential.

Revocable Attestations Putting Credentials in the Claimer's hands creates a new problem: since the Attester is no longer involved in the verification process, he cannot revoke Credential validity. In order to solve this problem, the KILT Blockchain stores a hash of the Attestation. On the KILT blockchain, attestations can be securely stored and time-stamped, which serves as a notary function and allows the revocation of credentials.

Four pieces of information are written onto the blockchain during an on-chain Attestation:

  • Public key of the Attester
  • Hash of the Claim that is being attested
  • Signature of the Attester
  • Placeholder field to mark if the Attestation is revoked later.

Credentials received from Claimants can be hashed by Verifiers and checked on the blockchain to see if there is an identical hash. By doing this, they can verify that the action was taken. Attesters have the right to revoke the blockchain hash, thus invalidating it. Blockchain verifyors will discover the revocation by checking the blockchain hash.

This process can also be applied to Legitimation and Delegation of Trust (which will be described later).

No information about the Claim is revealed, and it is not made publicly accessible on the blockchain. Attester's public key is stored on the chain. Verifiers use this property to determine if an Attester can be trusted.

Payment Transactions KILT Coin will be the cryptocurrency for the KILT blockchain. KILT Coins are used to encourage security and continuous network operation. Furthermore, the KILT Coin is used to register CTYPEs, issue Attestations, and revoke licenses on the blockchain. Paying the Attester for the Attestation service is done with the KILT Coin.

Building Top-Down Structures in KILT

Due to the permissionless nature of KILT, anyone can become an Attester and provide attestations. The KILT network is designed to remove barriers to being part of the system and embodying any role. Therefore, Claimers and Verifiers know which Attester to trust. Thus, Verifiers accept a Claim based on the trust they place in Attesters.

Trust between a Verifier and an Attester can be gained in different ways: The Verifier can directly trust an Attester. A second method of gaining trust is through legitimation or delegation from another Attester, and the Verifiers' trust in the Attester is based upon a specific trust structure, which models real-life trust relationships.

Top-down trust refers to trust between entities based on their status, reputation, or just their definition.

There is a legitimation that enables another entity to obtain specific permissions (capabilities) from a trusted source. Here is the simplest and most general example: Attester A1 legitimizes A2's arbitrary claim by attesting it. This attested claim (legitimation) is then used by Attester A2 to serve Claimer C.

The Claimer must check the legitimation of the Attester when requesting an attestation. Verifiers should also check that the legitimation chain is complete (i.e. none of the links have been revoked) before validating a credential. In order to ensure that the legitimation chain is valid, the Claimer and Verifier must be able to access all the necessary information.

Hierarchy of Trust

In a Hierarchy of Trust, the KILT Validator Nodes verify the complete authorization or delegation chain for certifying a CTYPE. By implementing this concept for KILT, trust can be delegated to other entities and a hierarchy of trust (i.e. a CTYPE) will be built around specific use cases. From the root, entities can determine who has the right to issue attestations for certain CTYPEs.

It can either be inherited directly or indirectly by the attester from a trusted source.

Structures are always stored and updated on the KILT blockchain (via delegation transactions). Since the delegation chains are stored on-chain, they are always available to the Validator Nodes. In other words, the Validator Nodes write an attestation backed by delegation to the chain only if the delegation chain is valid at the time of attestation. As a result, the Verifier is able to verify that the delegation chain is valid if the attestation is valid.

There might be a situation where an Employee loses the right to attest the specific CTYPE of the Hierarchy (e.g. leaves a company), so this should be reflected in the Authorization path. Upon revocation of Employee 1's authorization to certify the University Degree CTYPE by the Department, Employee 1 receives a revocation of the authorization. When Employee 1 tries to issue an attestation for this CTYPE and forge a path of authorization, the Validators will not write the attestation on the blockchain. Furthermore, the Claimer could present a forged off-chain attestation, but the Verifier would not be able to find it when he checked the authorization chain on the KILT blockchain. Previously attested documents remain valid after the revocation as the revocation date is later than the date of the attestation.

web3name

There is a one-to-one relationship between DIDs and web3names. This means that a KILT DID can be linked to only one web3name, and a web3name can be claimed only by one DID.

With web3name, you can now replace a DID string like this:

did:kilt:4s8kEBWV9nwU5znxmfR7DVA31DdBoqYGr49SAiAPzzigwJDm

with this:

w3n:ingo

Sporran

Sporran is the KILT wallet.

As opposed to crypto wallets, it can also create DIDs and store them on the KILT blockchain. Credentials can be requested, stored, maintained, signed, and shared.

Creating DIDs with the Sporran

  • User installs Sporran extensions and creates a KILT account
  • A local DID can be used for cryptographic challenges
  • This DID can be anchored on the KILT blockchain, making it accessible from other blockchains.

The Sporran is a browser extension for Firefox and Chrome that acts as a wallet for KILT logins and KILT coins. You will then receive your first DID. Digital identities include credentials such as email accounts, Twitter accounts, passports, and certificates.

SocialKYC

An account opening with a bank requires you to prove that you own your government-issued credentials, such as your passport. This process is known as KYC or "Know Your Customer." SocialKYC verifies a user's identity by verifying control of their social media accounts, email addresses, and phone numbers. SocialKYC doesn't remember the user or the credential after issuing a credential, unlike Web 2.0 sign-in processes. There is no storage, siloing, or sharing of personal data. Using SocialKYC and Sporran, users can manage and store their personal credentials and control what personal information they share online.

SocialKYC has a completely different approach to sensitive personal data than Web 2.0 systems. Users' credentials will be forgotten if the system provides them.

  • It begins with SocialKYC asking users to verify their email addresses and tweet a confirmation message to prove this is their account. Friend).
  • Once the user passes the SocialKYC check, the credential is issued. In their Sporran wallet, users can store credentials. A credential identifies who owns a particular account.
  • SocialKYC does not store or pass on this personal information. Under the user's control, it stays in their sporran wallet.
  • In order to use the Sporran wallet, users simply need to submit their credentials to any service that accepts them.

Conclusion

Specifically, the feature of linking several Web2 IDs is intriguing, due to the fact that entities that have proven ownership of several "reputable" Web2 IDs should be able to rely on them to give legitimacy to their Web3 identity. Moreover, a tool that allows us to prove ownership is important in our context. A tool such as KILT could be part of such a system.
